Research

These questions accumulated through building systems and watching them meet reality. Each time, the technical problem wasn't actually the hard part. The tldr: what's underneath it tends not to be tractable.

Being from Mauritius, growing up in Singapore, navigating three languages that carry different assumptions about how the world works — none of that makes me the most qualified person to answer what follows. But it somehow shapes what I notice. How you ask a question depends on which world you're standing in.

Currently working in trade and economic policy, where data and diplomacy meet. The question that keeps surfacing: when a system fails someone (particularly the someone it was never designed for) what recourse do they actually have? I'm not sure the answer is a better tool.

I. AI & Augmented Work

When AI interprets someone's emotional experience, whose philosophical frame is it using?

Most conversations about AI flatten into productivity metrics. But tools reshape cognition, not just output, and the assumptions baked into how a system infers are rarely made visible. The OTR Copilot project taught me that the NLP was the easy part; the harder problem was whose emotional expressions the system was built to read, and whose it wasn't. The question that followed: do LLMs carry recognisable philosophical priors in therapeutic contexts — defaulting toward individual agency, reframing, resolution — and what does that mean for seekers whose relationship to distress doesn't fit that frame?

II. Governance, Ethics & Accountability

When a system causes harm to the people it wasn't designed for, what mechanisms exist for recourse?

Technical design choices encode values: about who the user is, what counts as distress, and whose framework for recovery applies. Most of the time, those choices are invisible until something goes wrong. The question that follows from the AI work is structural and legal: when a system fails someone, particularly the someone it was never designed for, what accountability exists? Working through that means reading across legal philosophy, AI governance literature, and the question of what adequate recourse actually looks like.

III. Belonging & Social Infrastructure

What does it cost to be the person the system doesn't have a box for?

Belonging is treated as a given — something you either have or find. But for people who grow up between cultures, languages, and systems of meaning, it is more like a practice than a destination. Singapore is an interesting place to sit with this: a cosmopolitan city that is also a very legible racial taxonomy, where you can be surrounded by people and still be categorically alone. Kura was five years of trying to build something for that space: $50K raised, 2,500 users, and pilots with NUS and Duke-NUS. Closing it taught me things shipping it didn't.
