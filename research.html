Research

I think what shifted wasn't one moment. It was more like accumulating evidence. Watching the people building these tools — myself included — put up this veneer of tech for good while quietly optimizing for retention, or engagement, or whatever metric the grant required. Watching my computing cohort dismiss philosophy and history like those were elective concerns. Realizing that these are the people writing the systems. And underneath all of it, this memory from secondary school: a girl brought me to a room I didn't know existed, where the kids who couldn't move to get pencils were. Nobody was building an app for that. And I don't think an app was what they needed.

I don't have a tribe. That's the real answer. My friends have Chinese New Year, Korean student societies, somewhere to go back to. I have Mauritius, which is to say I have a place that doesn't map cleanly onto anything Singapore recognizes. NRIC lists my race as "MAURITIAN" — bureaucracy's way of saying we don't have a box for you. I can consume French media and Mandarin media, I look South Asian, I don't celebrate any of the festivities. I grew up inside that ambiguity. I think it's why I keep noticing the people the system didn't account for — because I know what it feels like to not be accounted for.

Currently working in trade and economic policy, somewhere at the edge of where data and diplomacy meet. The question that keeps not getting answered: when a system fails someone — specifically the someone it was never designed for — what do they actually do? I'm starting to think the answer isn't a better tool.

I. AI & augmented work

When a tool does the inferring, whose assumptions does it carry?

Most conversations about AI in the workplace flatten into productivity metrics: how much faster, how much cheaper. But tools reshape cognition, not just output. What happens to judgment, pattern recognition, and expertise when a tool starts doing the inferring is underexplored. Particularly for people early in their careers, who are asked to adopt these tools before they've developed the instincts the tools are meant to augment.

II. Economic power & how the world is structured

What happens when the framework for understanding a problem is itself part of the problem?

Economics gets taught as though its frameworks are neutral. They aren't; they encode assumptions about what counts as value, who gets to accumulate it, and what the state's role in that process should be. The trade wars, supply chain disruptions, and industrial policy debates of recent years have made those assumptions visible in ways they weren't before. Understanding them requires sitting with heterodox and structural economics alongside the mainstream.

III. Belonging & social infrastructure

What does it cost to be the person the system doesn't have a box for?

Belonging is treated as a given, something you either have or find. But for people who grow up between cultures, languages, and systems of meaning, it's more like a practice than a destination. Singapore is an interesting place to sit with this: a cosmopolitan city that is also a very legible racial taxonomy, where you can be surrounded by people and still be categorically alone.
