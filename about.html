About

Mauritius — my parents' home, and the place that has always complicated simple questions about where I'm from.

Research Interests

I build AI systems for people who live between languages and cultures, because I've spent my whole life doing that translation work myself.

Here's the kind of problem I'm interested in: A migrant worker arrives in Singapore, needs to set up a bank account and understand employment contracts, but the financial system assumes you already speak English and understand how CPF works. A youth mentor is supporting someone in crisis over text and needs to figure out if this is something they can handle or if it needs escalation, but the line between "I'm stressed about exams" and "I need professional intervention" isn't always obvious in digital communication, especially across cultural contexts.

At Over The Rainbow, I've been building emotion recognition systems for youth mental health support — working through what AI should and shouldn't do when someone's in crisis. With Reach Alliance, I built multilingual dialogue systems to get banking information to migrant workers across language barriers. And I've been researching how conversational AI shapes trust when people are navigating systems that weren't built for them.

The questions I keep coming back to: Where should technology actually step in versus where does it just get in the way? When someone's in crisis, what's the right escalation response, and how do you build systems that support human judgment rather than replacing it?

The more I work on these systems, the more I realize the hard questions aren't technical. When an algorithm flags someone's communication as risky or affects their access to services, who advocates for them? What recourse do they have? The gap I keep thinking about is between building better technology and making sure people can actually push back when it doesn't work for them.

I'm trying to figure out what it means to build technology that doesn't require people to flatten who they are in order to use it. That means co-design work where affected communities actually shape what gets built — and thinking seriously about what comes after, when the system fails or becomes its own kind of barrier.

Background

I've always existed between categories. Born to Mauritian parents but raised in Singapore, I grew up looking "brown" but speaking French and Mandarin, a combination that constantly confused people's attempts to place me. My NRIC lists my race as "MAURITIAN," which is about as close as bureaucracy gets to saying "we don't have a box for you." That experience of never quite belonging to one place, always translating between worlds, shaped how I think about technology and who it's built for.

I studied Information Systems at Singapore Management University and spent a semester at University College London. I've co-founded a mental health tech startup and researched conversational AI for development policy. I work across English, Mandarin, and French — that's not incidental to the research, it's half of what makes it possible.

I've lived and worked in Singapore, Beijing, and London. When I build multilingual AI systems, I'm drawing on a lifetime of switching between languages and registers. I notice the gaps because I've spent years navigating them.

Current Focus

I currently work in trade analytics, looking at how data, economics, and international commerce fit together. On the side, I volunteer with Over The Rainbow on youth mental health support systems, train with American Dragons Singapore for dragon boat, and stay connected to my previous research with Reach Alliance on conversational AI for migrant worker financial literacy.
