From the Red Dot to the Big Apple: Notes on Friendship and Belonging

August 2025 5 min read

I spent a week on New York's Upper West Side, discovering the intimate version of the city that tourists rarely see. The one where people at TJ Maxx actually chat with the clerks about their day. Where the bodega guy remembers your order. Where a sprawling metropolis somehow shrinks into a neighborhood.

I was staying with Arin, who's spent five years building a life here since leaving Singapore. We've been friends for longer than that, but there's something about watching someone construct an existence 10,000 kilometers from home that deepens things. Twelve time zones apart, and somehow the distance makes the connection feel closer. Maybe it's the intentionality required—you don't maintain friendships across oceans by accident.

One afternoon, we took the subway out to Brooklyn to find Hainan Jones—a chicken rice stall operated by two Americans with absolutely no Southeast Asian background. They'd never been to Singapore. They'd learned the dish from YouTube videos and sheer audacity. And honestly? It was pretty good.

Only in New York. Only in a city where authenticity matters less than audacity, where you can reinvent anything—including someone else's national dish—and people will line up to try it. The fact that it exists at all feels like a thesis statement about the place.

Walking through Manhattan, I kept thinking about scale. The skyscrapers don't just dwarf you—they somehow elevate you too. Your problems feel simultaneously insignificant and important. Your mess just becomes part of the bigger beautiful mess. There's a strange comfort in that.

What strikes me most about New York is what it doesn't ask. It doesn't ask where you're from. It doesn't ask if you deserve to be here. It doesn't care about your credentials or your history or the particular path that brought you to this corner at this moment. It only asks one thing: what are you going to do now that you are?

The city accommodates everyone's ambitions—fashion designers, actors, entrepreneurs, writers, dreamers of every variety. It treats individual struggles as part of its larger urban chaos. You're not special, but you're not unwelcome either. You just are.

After a week, I left with something electric still humming under my skin. Already planning when I'll be back. Still processing what it means to feel like you belong somewhere you've barely begun to know.

Maybe that's the real gift of places like this. Not the landmarks or the pizza or even the chicken rice. It's the permission to become someone you haven't been yet. The space to construct a self without having to explain where the pieces came from.

Arin figured that out five years ago. I'm still learning.
